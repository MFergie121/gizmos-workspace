# FIT2179 Visualisation Narrative — 11 Chart Version

## Project title
**Does Home Ground Advantage Actually Exist in the AFL?**

---

## Opening title / intro

Home-ground advantage is one of the most common assumptions in sport. In the AFL, teams are often said to benefit from playing at home because they travel less, know the venue better, and perform more comfortably in familiar conditions. But how real is that advantage, and what actually causes it?

This visualisation explores that question in three stages: first by proving whether home-ground advantage exists, then by examining the mechanisms that might explain it, and finally by asking whether the AFL’s biggest match — the Grand Final — is truly played on neutral terms.

---

# ACT 1 — PROVING THE ADVANTAGE EXISTS

## Chart 1 — League-Wide Home vs Away Win Rate

### Suggested title
**Do AFL teams actually win more often at home?**

### Narrative
The first step is to test the basic claim. Across all team-games from 2010 to 2024, home teams win more often than away teams.

This establishes the foundation for the rest of the visualisation: home-ground advantage is not just a cliché or fan mythology. At the league-wide level, there is a measurable difference between playing at home and playing away.

### Takeaway
Home-ground advantage appears to be real at the broadest league-wide level.

### Transition
But a single average can hide a lot. If this advantage is real, the next question is whether it has remained consistent across time — or whether it has weakened as travel, facilities, and professionalism have improved.

---

## Chart 2 — Home Advantage Over Time

### Suggested title
**Has home-ground advantage stayed consistent over time?**

### Narrative
Looking season by season, the home advantage effect remains visible across the modern AFL era. While the gap may fluctuate slightly from year to year, the overall pattern remains stable: home teams continue to outperform away teams.

This matters because it suggests the effect is not an isolated historical quirk. Even in a highly professional national competition, location still matters.

### Takeaway
Home-ground advantage is not just real — it is persistent.

### Transition
However, league averages still don’t tell us where that advantage is strongest. To understand that, we need to look at specific clubs and specific grounds.

---

## Chart 3 — Fortress Matrix (Team × Venue Win % Heatmap)

### Suggested title
**Which AFL grounds are true fortresses?**

### Narrative
This heatmap reveals that not all venues are equal. Some clubs show exceptionally strong win rates at particular grounds, creating what can be described as fortress venues.

That pattern suggests home-ground advantage is not evenly distributed across the league. Instead, it is concentrated in certain club-venue combinations where familiarity, travel burden on opponents, and local conditions may combine to create a stronger edge.

### Takeaway
Some home grounds create far stronger advantages than others.

### Transition
If some venues are fortresses, then some clubs should also show a bigger gap between home and away performance. The next chart compares that gap directly.

---

## Chart 4 — Home Win % vs Away Win % by Club

### Suggested title
**Which clubs are genuinely stronger at home?**

### Narrative
This chart compares each club’s average home win percentage with its away win percentage. The gap between the two shows how much each club appears to benefit from home conditions.

Some clubs display a very large home-away difference, while others are more balanced. This confirms that home-ground advantage is uneven. It is not a fixed league-wide bonus that applies equally to everyone.

### Takeaway
Home-ground advantage exists, but some clubs benefit far more from it than others.

### Transition
At this point, the existence of home-ground advantage is fairly clear. The more difficult question is why it happens. Is it mainly caused by travel? By climate? By venue familiarity? The next section tests those explanations.

---

# ACT 2 — EXPLAINING THE CAUSES

## Chart 5 — Distance Travelled vs Win Percentage

### Suggested title
**Does travel burden actually reduce performance?**

### Narrative
A common explanation for home-ground advantage is travel. Interstate teams, especially those based far from Victoria, often travel much more than Victorian clubs over the course of a season.

This chart tests that idea by comparing total travel distance with win percentage. The pattern suggests that travel matters, but not in a simple or perfectly linear way. Some teams travel enormous distances and still perform strongly, while others do not.

### Takeaway
Travel contributes to the story, but distance alone does not fully explain home-ground advantage.

### Transition
If kilometres travelled are not the whole answer, then geography may be shaping performance in broader ways. The next chart steps back from individual clubs and looks at state-level patterns.

---

## Chart 6 — State-by-State Performance Comparison

### Suggested title
**Do some states begin with a structural advantage?**

### Narrative
This chart compares average performance by state. Victorian clubs often benefit from reduced travel demands and greater access to shared metropolitan venues, while interstate clubs generally face heavier travel loads.

However, state-level performance does not map neatly onto a simple advantage-disadvantage story. Some interstate clubs remain highly competitive despite the extra burden, suggesting that geography shapes context but does not determine outcomes on its own.

### Takeaway
Geography matters, but it does not act alone.

### Transition
That points toward another mechanism: familiarity. If home-ground advantage is partly about knowing the venue better than your opponent, then the effect should weaken when the opponent is also comfortable there.

---

## Chart 7 — True Home vs Shared Home Comparison

### Suggested title
**Is home advantage weaker when the venue is shared?**

### Narrative
This chart compares matches played in a “true home” context against matches played in a “shared home” context. For example, a Victorian club playing an interstate side at the MCG may enjoy a clearer familiarity advantage than when playing another MCG tenant.

If the home side’s success rate drops in shared-home scenarios, that suggests the venue advantage is not just about being officially listed as the home team. It is also about whether the opponent is unfamiliar, uncomfortable, or disadvantaged in that environment.

### Takeaway
Home-ground advantage is stronger when venue familiarity is one-sided.

### Transition
Familiarity is not only about the ground itself. In the AFL, teams also move between very different climates. That raises the possibility that environmental conditions contribute to the advantage as well.

---

## Chart 8 — Average Temperature by City & Month

### Suggested title
**Does the AFL really play under one set of conditions?**

### Narrative
This heatmap shows average temperatures across AFL host cities during the main season months. The variation is clear: teams do not compete in one neutral environmental setting.

Cities such as Perth, Brisbane, and the Gold Coast can present very different conditions from Melbourne or Hobart, especially during winter months. That means travelling teams may be adjusting not just to a new venue, but to a different physical environment.

### Takeaway
AFL matches are played across clearly different climates, not one neutral national setting.

### Transition
If climate differences are real, the next question is whether they actually affect results.

---

## Chart 9 — Climate-Split Home/Away Performance

### Suggested title
**Does environmental familiarity strengthen home advantage?**

### Narrative
This chart compares performance under more familiar versus less familiar climate conditions. The purpose is to test whether teams perform differently when they play in environments that are closer to or further from their usual home conditions.

If home teams perform better when the climate difference is greater, or away teams perform worse when travelling into unfamiliar conditions, then climate becomes a plausible part of the explanation for home-ground advantage.

### Takeaway
Home-ground advantage may be partly environmental, not just geographic.

### Transition
By this point, the story is more nuanced. Travel matters, but not enough on its own. Venue familiarity appears important, and climate may intensify the effect. The final section asks what happens when those same patterns are embedded into the AFL’s most important game.

---

# ACT 3 — FROM HOME ADVANTAGE TO STRUCTURAL ADVANTAGE

## Chart 10 — Grand Final Winners with MCG Highlight

### Suggested title
**Is the Grand Final really played on neutral ground?**

### Narrative
The Grand Final is often framed as the ultimate neutral contest — the best two teams meeting on the biggest stage. But in practice, the AFL Grand Final is usually played at the MCG, a venue that some clubs know far better than others.

This chart highlights how often winning teams were already MCG-familiar or effectively MCG-based. That does not prove every result was unfair, but it raises an important question: when the venue consistently favours one type of club, can it really be called neutral?

### Takeaway
At Grand Final level, venue familiarity starts to look less like a natural sporting factor and more like a structural one.

### Transition
To see whether this is just anecdotal, the final chart steps back and looks at the long-run pattern.

---

## Chart 11 — Grand Final Timeline: Victorian vs Interstate Winners

### Suggested title
**Who has benefited most from the Grand Final structure over time?**

### Narrative
This timeline shows whether Grand Final winners were Victorian clubs, interstate clubs, or the special COVID-era neutral-venue exceptions. Over time, the pattern helps reveal whether the MCG-centred system aligns with repeated advantages for particular kinds of clubs.

Even though interstate teams have certainly broken through, the broader structure still appears to reward clubs already familiar with the default Grand Final venue.

### Takeaway
The Grand Final suggests that home-ground advantage in the AFL can become structural, not just situational.

---

# Final Conclusion

## Suggested title
**So, does home-ground advantage actually exist in the AFL?**

## Closing narrative
The evidence suggests that the answer is yes — but not for just one reason.

Home-ground advantage exists at the league-wide level, but it is uneven across clubs and venues. Travel helps explain part of the effect, but not all of it. Venue familiarity appears to be a stronger explanation in some contexts, and climate may further strengthen the edge for certain teams.

Most importantly, the Grand Final shows that home-ground advantage can shift from a normal feature of sport into something more structural. When the same venue repeatedly favours clubs already familiar with it, the question is no longer just whether home teams perform better — it is whether the competition itself is fully neutral at its highest stage.

## Final message
Home-ground advantage in the AFL is real — but its strongest forms come from a mix of geography, environmental familiarity, and competition structure.
